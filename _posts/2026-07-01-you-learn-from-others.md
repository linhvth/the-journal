I always thought I was an independent person. To some extent, that's still true. But I think I was over-independent, and I mistook it for character rather than a coping mechanism. The moment I sensed an obstacle coming, I would isolate myself, then anxiously and quietly "overcome" it in private and alone. I convinced myself that I had to solve it alone before I had earned the right to come back. Only once everything seemed resolved, or at least I'd convinced myself it was, would I resurface and tell my friends about it, casually, like it had been nothing at all.

Little did I know, I was really just lonely, fighting alone, without even one person to talk to in the middle of it.

And little did I know, even just the presence of someone (not their advice or fixing) would have made me feel seen and so much better about everything. It simply makes the burden feel less impossible to carry.

I was in DC in mid-May, traveling alone right after graduation and before moving back to Vietnam. Money was tight, so while I could afford the trip, I didn't have much flexibility. I booked the cheapest mixed-gender hostel, hoping there'd be a decent number of women. Turned out it was just me and one Filipina among 10 other men. When I checked in and realized that, I was disappointed, but I tried to talk myself into thinking it was fine, nothing to worry about. Still, I texted two friends about it, venting my discomfort. 

One of them — let's call him Ng — called me immediately and told me I needed to switch to a female-only Airbnb. I was exhausted after a long, hot day in DC. And I was overwhelmed and frustrated that changing accommodations meant spending more money than I had planned. On top of that, I have this strange reflex of resisting help, especially when it comes from men (that's probably something I'll have to unpack another day).

I got angry. I snapped at him, mid-search for a new place to stay, voice sharp and irritated. I tried to regain control over everything around me. He stayed on the phone with me for 2 hours anyway, the entire time it took me to find somewhere new and get there safely. At one point, something happened on the way that made him ask me to share my location, and he even checked out the neighborhood of the new place to make sure it was safe.

It wasn't until I was finally alone in that new room, door locked, breathing slower, that I realized (1) I had been angry and had projected my discomfort onto the one person trying to help me; and (2) even after that, he had stayed. Patient. Listening. Talking me down until I felt okay again.

And I didn't realize how much that meant to me until that exact moment. 
I thought I was independent because I could survive alone. I tried to scream “I can handle this myself”, but it was actually “I am not allowed to let anyone see me struggling”. Part of me struggles to receive care and becomes defensive when I feel vulnerable. But another part desperately needed to be seen and empathized with. 

I then believe that some parts of ourselves only exist around other people. I don't think I could have discovered those things by sitting alone in my room and reflecting. They only appeared because another person was there. The same is true for so many parts of who we are. I still think solitude matters, but it is not enough. Some parts of us only come alive in relationship. They don't exist in isolation because they require another person to call them into being.

I thought I knew myself. Turns out I didn't. And I have a feeling it'll be a long time before I truly do.
